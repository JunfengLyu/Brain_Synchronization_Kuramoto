Modeling Emergent Synchronization in Brain Networks

This page presents an interactive companion to a course project on synchronization, the Kuramoto model, and large-scale brain network dynamics. The first version contains the completed introduction and Kuramoto-model review, together with browser-based simulations adapted from the MATLAB demos.

Introduction to Synchronization Phenomenon

Synchronization originates from the Greek words syn (together) and chronos (time), describing a universal phenomenon in which interacting oscillators coordinate rhythms to a global frequency and phase. The scientific study of synchronization can be traced back to Huygens' observation of two pendulums coupled through a common support in the 17th century. Since then, synchronization has been identified and modeled through a common framework in physical, chemical, and biological systems.

Biological synchronization occurs across scales. Firefly swarms can produce synchronized flash bursts; cardiac tissue contracts through coordinated excitation; neural populations generate coherent electrical rhythms; and molecular circadian clocks such as the KaiABC system can maintain robust biochemical oscillations.

Brain synchronization is especially intriguing because neural systems organize across many spatial and temporal scales. Coordinated activity between neurons and brain regions may support information integration, communication, perception, and attention, while abnormal synchronization is related to disorders such as epilepsy, Parkinson's disease, and schizophrenia. Understanding how collective rhythms emerge from structural brain networks is therefore a central problem in contemporary neuroscience.

The Kuramoto model provides a compact phenomenological description of this phenomenon. Oscillators interact through a sinusoidal coupling term and undergo a transition from incoherence to synchronization as the coupling strength passes a critical threshold. In this project, the Kuramoto model is used first as a theoretical null model and then as a starting point for modeling synchronization on realistic brain networks.

Review on Kuramoto Model

Model Establishment

Consider $N$ oscillators with phases $\bm{\theta}=(\theta_1,\theta_2,\cdots,\theta_N)^\top$. Natural oscillators generally have a dispersed intrinsic-frequency distribution $g(\omega)$ near a central frequency $\bar{\omega}$; here we use $\omega_i\overset{\rm i.i.d.}{\sim}\mathcal{N}(\bar{\omega},\sigma^2)$.

The general phase dynamics can be written as

$$ \frac{d\theta_i}{dt}=\omega_i+\frac{K}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N \mathcal{F}(\theta_j-\theta_i), $$

where $K$ is the homogeneous coupling strength. Periodicity and exchange asymmetry motivate the sinusoidal approximation $\mathcal{F}(\Delta\theta)\simeq\sin\Delta\theta$, yielding the basic Kuramoto model

$$ \frac{d\theta_i}{dt}=\omega_i+\frac{K}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N \sin(\theta_j-\theta_i). $$

Emergent Synchronization and the Order Parameter

A standard order parameter summarizes the collective phase:

$$ \tilde{z}(t)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N e^{i\theta_j(t)} =R(t)e^{i\Theta(t)}. $$

The magnitude $R(t)$ measures synchronization. When $R\to 0$, phases are broadly dispersed; when $R\to 1$, oscillators form a coherent synchronized cluster.

Mean-Field Reduction

Under a mean-field approximation, the mutual interaction is replaced by an average interaction $R\sin(\bar{\omega}t-\theta_i)$:

$$ \frac{d\theta_i}{dt}\simeq \omega_i+KR\sin(\bar{\omega}t-\theta_i). $$

In the rotating coordinate $\phi_i=\theta_i-\bar{\omega}t$, this becomes

$$ \frac{d\phi_i}{dt}=(\omega_i-\bar{\omega})-KR\sin\phi_i :=\nu_i-KR\sin\phi_i. $$

For $|\nu_i|\le KR$, the reduced equation has a fixed point

$$ \phi_i^*=\arcsin\frac{\nu_i}{KR}\in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2},+\frac{\pi}{2}\right]. $$

These locked oscillators contribute coherently to the synchronized group, while oscillators with $|\nu_i|>KR$ drift relative to the mean field.

Self-Consistency and Bifurcation

Under the infinite-oscillator limit, a steady distribution concentrated at the locked fixed point gives the self-consistent equation

$$ R=KR\int_{-1}^{+1}g(\bar{\omega}+KRx)\sqrt{1-x^2}\,dx. $$

Equivalently, one can define

$$ \begin{aligned} \psi(R;K) &=\left\{\sum_{t=0}^{+\infty} \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} \frac{\Gamma(t+\frac{1}{2})}{(t+1)!(2t)!} g^{(2t)}(\bar{\omega})K^{2t+1}R^{2t}\right\}-1\\ &\simeq \left(\frac{\pi g(\bar{\omega})}{2}K-1\right) +\frac{\pi g''(\bar{\omega})}{16}K^3R^2+\mathcal{O}(R^4)=0. \end{aligned} $$

Since $g''(\bar{\omega})<0$ for a Gaussian peak, a nonzero solution appears when

$$ K_c=\frac{2}{\pi g(\bar{\omega})}, \qquad R^*\sim(K-K_c)^{1/2}. $$
