Modeling the Emergent Synchronization of Brain Networks

Synchronization is a universal phenomenon observed in systems ranging from firefly swarms to circadian clocks and neuronal populations. Here we investigate large-scale brain synchronization using the Kuramoto model on structural connectomes derived from the AAL atlas and ADNI-related cohorts.

The project connects mean-field Kuramoto theory, network topology, empirical brain connectomes, Alzheimer's disease progression, and circadian resynchronization. The page below combines the report text, final figures, browser simulations, and minimal reproducible code.

Introduction to Synchronization Phenomenon

Synchronization describes the tendency of interacting oscillators to coordinate rhythms to a global frequency and phase. Its scientific study can be traced to Huygens' 17th-century observation of coupled pendulums and now spans physical, chemical, and biological systems.

Biological synchronization appears across scales: firefly swarms generate synchronized flashes, cardiac tissue contracts coherently, neural populations produce collective rhythms, and molecular circadian clocks maintain robust daily cycles. Brain synchronization is especially important because coordinated activity may support communication, perception, and attention, while abnormal synchronization is linked to neurological disorders.

Review on Kuramoto Model

For $N$ oscillators with phases $\theta_i$, the classical Kuramoto model is

$$ \frac{d\theta_i}{dt}=\omega_i+\frac{K}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}\sin(\theta_j-\theta_i). $$

Collective synchronization is described by the complex order parameter

$$ \tilde z(t)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}e^{i\theta_j(t)}=R(t)e^{i\Theta(t)}. $$

The magnitude $R(t)$ measures coherence: $R\to0$ indicates incoherence, while $R\to1$ indicates phase locking.

In the rotating frame $\phi_i=\theta_i-\bar\omega t$, mean-field theory gives

$$ \frac{d\phi_i}{dt}=\nu_i-KR\sin\phi_i. $$

Fixed points exist when $|\nu_i|\le KR$, explaining how the synchronized subgroup emerges as coupling increases.

For a Gaussian frequency distribution, the self-consistency equation produces the critical coupling

$$ K_c=\frac{2}{\pi g(\bar\omega)},\qquad R^*\sim(K-K_c)^{1/2}. $$

Heterogeneous network coupling replaces the all-to-all interaction with a connection matrix:

$$ \frac{d\theta_i}{dt}=\omega_i+\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}K_{ij}\sin(\theta_j-\theta_i). $$

Global, Erdős-Rényi, and Barabási-Albert networks reveal how hubs reduce the coupling threshold for synchronization.

Connection Architecture Based on the AAL Atlas Connectome

The empirical brain network uses the AAL-90 atlas, where each macroscopic brain region is modeled as an oscillator. Subject-level structural matrices are symmetrized, self-connections are removed, and a 40% population consensus rule retains edges present across a sufficient fraction of subjects.

Node degree is used to identify structural super hubs above the 85th percentile. These hubs become the targets for later perturbation and rescue experiments.

Modeling Synchronization of Brain Networks

The AAL network is injected into a stochastic Kuramoto equation:

$$ d\theta_i=\omega_i\,dt+\lambda\sum_{j=1}^{N}M_{ij}\sin(\theta_j-\theta_i)\,dt+\sigma\,dW_i. $$

The global order parameter $R$ and edge synchronization $R_{link}$ quantify macroscopic coherence and structural-edge phase locking.

Module-wise simulations show that local synchronization emerges before global coherence. The top structural hubs lock earlier than regular modules and act as a dynamical anchor for whole-brain integration.

Targeted frequency perturbation experiments compare hub nodes, random peripheral nodes, and the frontal module. Hubs entrain the rest of the network at the lowest critical coupling, demonstrating strong topological leverage.

Alzheimer's Disease Continuum

Group-average structural connectomes are built for CN, EMCI, LMCI, and AD cohorts. Topological regression tests whether regions with high centrality, clustering, local efficiency, or participation coefficient are preferentially affected by structural degradation.

Kuramoto simulations show a subtle but consistent rightward shift of the phase transition along disease progression. The AD network requires elevated coupling to reach comparable global coherence.

Resynchronization Dynamics of Circadian Brain Oscillators

A forced Kuramoto model describes SCN circadian oscillators under external light-dark forcing:

$$ \frac{d\theta_i}{dt}=\omega_i+\frac{K}{N}\sum_j\sin(\theta_j-\theta_i)+F\sin(\sigma t-\theta_i+p(t)). $$

With a Lorentzian frequency distribution, the Ott-Antonsen reduction yields

$$ \dot{\tilde z}=\frac{1}{2}[(K\tilde z+F)-\tilde z^2(K\bar{\tilde z}+F)]-(\Delta+i\Omega)\tilde z. $$

Phase-space geometry explains east-west jet-lag asymmetry: saddle structures and separatrices can prolong recovery for specific phase advances.
